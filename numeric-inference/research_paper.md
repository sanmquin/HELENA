# Anisotropic Semantic Metrics for Numeric Prediction from Language

## Abstract

Numeric prediction from natural language is often treated as a problem of extracting a single latent meaning from text and mapping that meaning onto a scalar outcome. This paper studies a more structured alternative: title embeddings are not evaluated as isotropic semantic objects, but as anisotropic coordinates whose dimensions contribute differently to popularity prediction. In a YouTube view-prediction task, video titles were embedded, reduced to a 15-dimensional PCA space, and used to predict log views with per-channel linear models. Large language model prediction methods were then evaluated under four prompting regimes: global channel descriptions, dimension-aware prompts, feature-evaluation prompts in which the model labeled dimension contributions, and guided inference prompts that supplied qualitative hints derived from the most significant dimensions. The principal empirical result is that guided generation achieved the strongest LLM performance, with the lowest normalized MAE among LLM methods and competitive or superior channel-level performance relative to OLS on several channels. A second result is that better qualitative labeling of latent dimensions correlated with lower numeric prediction error, establishing a measurable link between representation accuracy and reasoning accuracy. A third result complicates the story: the strongest correlation with OLS numeric predictions came from significant-dimension prompting rather than guided generation, suggesting that LLM prediction may inherit linear-model structure more reliably than it improves scalar accuracy. These findings motivate anisotropic evaluation metrics for non-verifiable reasoning, where dimensions are weighted by their task relevance rather than treated equally as in cosine similarity.

## 1. Introduction: From Isotropic Similarity to Anisotropic Reasoning

Most semantic evaluation metrics begin from an isotropic assumption. A sentence embedding is treated as a point in a high-dimensional space, and the similarity between two texts is computed by applying the same rule to every coordinate. Cosine similarity is the canonical example. It asks whether two vectors point in the same direction, but it does not ask whether one direction matters more than another for a particular task. This is appropriate when the evaluator wants a broad measure of semantic relatedness. It is insufficient when the evaluator wants to predict a task-specific outcome.

A video title can be semantically similar to another title while differing along precisely the dimension that determines audience response. For one channel, geopolitical stakes may increase views; for another, niche technical specificity may improve performance; for a third, the same specificity may narrow the audience and reduce reach. The relevant geometry is therefore anisotropic. Dimensions do not contribute equally to the outcome. Some dimensions have a positive slope, some have a negative slope, some are irrelevant, and some matter only inside a channel-specific context. A metric that weights every direction equally collapses these distinctions.

The central idea of this paper is that prompts can function as task-specific metric operators. When a model is asked to reason about a title, the prompt does not merely provide background information. It selects a topology of relevance. It tells the model which regions of semantic space matter, which dimensions should be emphasized, which signs should be interpreted as beneficial or detrimental, and which channel-specific regularities should override generic intuitions. In this sense, prompting plays a role analogous to test-time reasoning: it transforms a general semantic representation into a task-conditioned evaluation geometry.

This perspective connects numeric prediction to a broader problem in cognition and language. Human reasoning is not performed over neutral facts alone; it is shaped by interference from prior context, salient examples, memory traces, and competing associations. Proactive interference in the human brain describes a situation in which previously learned information disrupts the encoding or retrieval of new information. A similar phenomenon can arise in language models. General associations about what should be popular may interfere with a specific channel's empirical audience behavior. Anisotropic prompts can be interpreted as a way of reducing this interference by making the relevant axes explicit.

The experiment studied here predicts YouTube video popularity from title semantics. The task is numeric and probabilistic rather than categorical. Exact prediction is impossible because views depend on exogenous variables such as recommendation dynamics, timing, guests, thumbnails, platform behavior, and channel history. Yet title semantics still contain measurable predictive information. The goal is therefore not to prove that language alone determines views, but to examine whether semantic dimensions can be weighted, described, and used to improve numeric inference.

## 2. Methodology

The workflow followed the ordered notebooks in the numeric-inference directory. It began with data construction, proceeded through embedding and linear modeling, generated qualitative descriptions of latent dimensions, evaluated multiple LLM prediction regimes, and concluded with a comparative results analysis. The design intentionally mirrors a layered reasoning pipeline: first estimate the task geometry statistically, then translate that geometry into language, then test whether the language model can use the translated geometry to make better predictions.

The dataset builder collected structured YouTube channel data containing channel identifiers, channel names, video titles, video identifiers, publication metadata, and view counts. The numeric modeling notebook then filtered the dataset to channels with sufficient data from 2023 through 2025, leaving 17 channels and 7,044 videos. Each video title was embedded with a sentence-transformer model, and the dataset was split into per-channel training and testing partitions. The resulting split contained 5,629 training embeddings and 1,415 testing embeddings.

Dimensionality reduction was then applied to convert the original 384-dimensional embedding space into a 15-dimensional PCA representation. These dimensions should not be interpreted as clean human concepts by default. They are latent axes that summarize directions of variance in the title-embedding distribution. The reduced space explained 29.52% of embedding variance, which is enough to preserve a meaningful low-dimensional semantic skeleton while forcing a strong compression of the original embedding geometry.

For each channel, an ordinary least squares model was trained to predict log-transformed views from the 15 PCA dimensions. The target was `log1p(views)`, which reduces the impact of extreme viral outliers and makes errors interpretable on a logarithmic scale. Each channel received its own model because audience behavior is channel-specific. A semantic direction that predicts success for one channel may predict failure for another. The OLS coefficient vector therefore defines an anisotropic metric for that channel: each dimension receives a signed weight, and the predicted outcome is the sum of those weighted contributions plus an intercept.

The first LLM stage translated the statistical geometry into language. The pipeline generated global performance descriptions for each channel by contrasting top-performing and bottom-performing videos. It also generated MECE-style descriptions of the 15 PCA dimensions by comparing titles with strongly positive and strongly negative values along each dimension. Finally, it generated channel-specific explanations of the most significant dimensions, using the statistically strongest latent axes for each channel as the basis for qualitative reasoning.

The next stage evaluated four LLM prediction methodologies. The first methodology, global prediction, provided the model with high-level channel success drivers and asked it to predict log views for test titles. This method tested whether broad qualitative knowledge of a channel's audience was sufficient for numeric inference. The second methodology, dimension prediction, provided dimension descriptions and PCA-related information, testing whether explicit latent-coordinate context improved the model's alignment with the OLS semantic structure. The third methodology, feature-evaluation prediction, asked the model to label the contribution of relevant dimensions using an ordered seven-point scale ranging from extremely negative to extremely positive, and then to predict log views. This method made the model's intermediate representation auditable: if the model misunderstood a dimension, that misunderstanding could be compared to its numeric error. The fourth methodology, guided inference, supplied qualitative hints derived directly from the five most significant dimensions for each channel. Instead of asking the model to infer all latent contributions from scratch, the prompt gave it signed semantic evidence and asked it to convert that evidence into a numeric prediction.

The final results analysis aligned predictions across 986 shared test videos from the 10 most statistically significant channels. The comparison included the four LLM methods, the OLS numeric model, and mean and median baselines. Because the channels differed greatly in absolute view scale, the analysis used normalized error measures for cross-channel comparison while also preserving log-view metrics for model-level interpretation.

## 3. Results

The OLS baseline established that title embeddings contain predictive signal, but also that the problem remains noisy. Across all 17 tested channels, the global OLS test R2 was 0.6638 and the global log-scale MAE was 0.9161. The mean per-channel test R2 was lower, at 0.1355, showing that aggregate performance was stronger than typical channel-level explanatory power. This gap is important. It indicates that a low-dimensional title representation captures broad popularity structure, but that channel-level prediction remains difficult and uneven.

The first key result is that guided generation achieved the best LLM outcome. In the aligned 10-channel comparison, guided inference produced the lowest overall normalized MAE among the LLM methods. Its mean normalized error was lower than global prompting, dimension prompting, and feature-evaluation prompting, and it was competitive with the numeric model and baselines. At the channel level, guided inference outperformed or closely matched OLS in several cases, including Asianometry, a16z, Greg Isenberg, and Peter H. Diamandis. The result supports the paper's central claim: numerical inference, including probabilistic reasoning about uncertain outcomes, can be improved by data-analysis insights when those insights are translated into the prompt.

This does not mean that the LLM became a better linear regressor than OLS in every setting. Rather, guided inference improved the model's usable reasoning context. It reduced the burden of rediscovering the relevant semantic axes and supplied a channel-specific interpretation of the title. The model still had to map qualitative evidence onto a scalar log-view prediction, but it no longer had to infer the most important dimensions without support. In topological terms, the guided prompt provided a task-conditioned coordinate chart.

The second key result is that better dimension labeling correlated with better numeric prediction. In the feature-evaluation notebook, the model labeled semantic dimension contributions on an ordered seven-point scale. The global mean absolute label error was 1.546 ranks, exact-match accuracy was 20.8%, and neighbor accuracy within one rank was 55.0%. At the video level, the correlation between mean label error and absolute prediction error was positive and statistically significant: r = 0.1187 with p = 2.146e-04. Videos with accurate labels had lower mean prediction error than videos with inaccurate labels.

This finding is important because it suggests that reasoning accuracy can be estimated through latent-representation accuracy. In many realistic prediction tasks, ground truth is unavailable, delayed, noisy, or expensive. A marketing team may not know future sales, an educator may not know future learning outcomes, and a communicator may not know future audience response. But the system may still be able to evaluate whether its intermediate representation is faithful to the relevant dimensions. If better labeling of dimensions correlates with better final prediction, then representation-level metrics can provide a proxy for expected reasoning quality before the final outcome is observed.

The third result is more subtle. The strongest correlation with numeric OLS predictions was achieved by dimension prompting, not guided inference. Across the aligned dataset, the dimension LLM had a correlation of 0.9118 with the OLS model, while guided inference had a correlation of 0.9024. Feature-evaluation predictions correlated at 0.8530, and global prompting correlated at 0.8325. Dimension prompting therefore tracked the linear model most closely even though guided inference achieved the better overall LLM error profile.

This creates an important research question. A model can be highly correlated with OLS while still producing biased or under-dispersed predictions. The results analysis found that the actual test-set variance was 4.4421, while OLS prediction variance was 3.0230 and guided prediction variance was 3.3931. LLM methods differed in bias and dispersion, with feature evaluation showing stronger positive bias and guided inference showing a smaller bias. The dimension-prompting method appears to inherit the structure of the OLS model especially well, but that structural alignment does not automatically yield the best calibrated predictions. The open question is whether LLM-generated predictions can improve beyond simple linear models by capturing nonlinear relationships, correcting variance compression, using channel-specific priors more flexibly, or introducing additional relevant dimensions not present in the PCA representation.

## 4. Discussion

The results support a view of prompting as anisotropic metric construction. In traditional embedding evaluation, all dimensions contribute symmetrically to similarity. In this experiment, the OLS coefficient vector breaks that symmetry. Each channel defines a different weighted semantic field. A title is not merely close or far from another title; it has positive and negative projections along dimensions that affect expected popularity. The LLM prompt then attempts to express that weighted field in language.

Guided inference performed best among the LLM methods because it made the anisotropic structure explicit. Instead of asking the model to infer whether a title was promising from generic semantic associations, the prompt supplied labels for the most significant dimensions. This resembles giving a human analyst not only the title, but also a structured diagnostic report: the title is strongly positive on a dimension associated with broad macro anxiety, mildly negative on a dimension associated with niche technical implementation, and neutral on another dimension. The analyst can still disagree with the model or adjust for context, but the reasoning process begins from a better-conditioned representation.

The label-accuracy result extends this interpretation. If the LLM incorrectly labels the latent dimensions, its prediction error tends to increase. This is a modest correlation, not a deterministic law, but its significance matters because the representation is upstream of the final scalar answer. A future system could use this structure to estimate its own reliability. If it can compare its qualitative labels to a statistical or learned representation of the relevant dimensions, it can identify when its reasoning trace is likely to be distorted. This is especially valuable in non-verifiable domains, where final correctness cannot be checked immediately.

The dimension-correlation result cautions against overclaiming. The strongest alignment with OLS came from significant-dimension predictions rather than guided inference. This suggests that when a prompt directly exposes the latent coordinate system, the LLM can reproduce the linear model's structure with high fidelity. But reproducing a linear model is not the same as improving on it. The next question is whether LLMs can add value where linear models are structurally limited. They may do so by modeling nonlinear interactions among dimensions, correcting for tail events, incorporating additional video-specific context such as named guests or timeliness, adjusting uncertainty based on title archetypes, or using richer dimension sets that are chosen per video rather than fixed per channel.

From a training perspective, these results suggest several improvement paths. One path is supervised fine-tuning on structured reasoning traces where the model must predict dimension labels before predicting outcomes. The target would not be the final number alone, but the full chain from title to latent-coordinate interpretation to signed contribution to scalar prediction. Another path is preference training in which candidate predictions are ranked not only by numeric error, but by consistency with the anisotropic semantic field. A third path is self-distillation: a larger model or statistical teacher generates dimension-aware explanations, and a smaller model learns to internalize the mapping from language to weighted dimensions.

The text-classification experiment provides an important comparison. There, the central metric was topological projection: generated category descriptions were evaluated according to whether they preserved the pairwise geometry of category centroids. The question was whether a description function mapped one finite semantic space into another without distorting neighborhoods. In the numeric prediction setting, the problem is different. The relevant metric is not only whether distances are preserved, but whether directions are weighted correctly. A dimension can be semantically real yet detrimental to the task. Another dimension can be weakly visible under cosine similarity yet highly predictive for the outcome. The text-classification metric evaluates structural fidelity between spaces; the numeric metric evaluates task-conditioned anisotropy within a space.

The applications extend beyond entertainment analytics. In sales, the same method could identify which semantic dimensions of a pitch, email, or product description predict conversion for a specific customer segment. In marketing, it could distinguish generic brand similarity from task-specific persuasion factors, such as urgency, credibility, aspiration, or perceived risk. In education, it could estimate whether lesson descriptions, practice prompts, or feedback messages align with dimensions that predict learning outcomes. In communication, it could help writers understand which aspects of a message increase clarity, trust, or memorability for a given audience. In personal self-development, it could support reflective systems that identify which framings of goals, habits, or plans correlate with follow-through.

The broader implication is that long-context generation and communication tasks should not be evaluated only by local semantic similarity. A long output has a trajectory. It begins from an intent, moves through claims and examples, changes emphasis, and eventually arrives at a conclusion or action. The next metric should therefore consider trajectory similarity. Instead of comparing two static point clouds or weighting fixed dimensions, trajectory metrics would compare paths through semantic space. A generated explanation, sales narrative, educational plan, or personal reflection could be evaluated according to whether it follows a productive trajectory: preserving relevant state, avoiding interference, moving through necessary intermediate concepts, and reaching an intended region of outcome space.

## 5. Limitations

The experiment is limited by the data and representation choices. YouTube views depend on many factors that are not contained in titles, including thumbnails, upload timing, guest recognition, platform recommendation dynamics, external events, subscriber notifications, and historical audience behavior. The title embedding is therefore an incomplete signal. The PCA representation also explains only part of the original embedding variance, and the interpretation of PCA dimensions remains approximate even when generated descriptions are useful.

The LLM methods were evaluated on the 10 most statistically significant channels, which makes the analysis appropriate for studying semantic signal but less representative of channels where title semantics have weak predictive power. The feature-labeling correlation is statistically significant but modest, so it should be understood as evidence of a relationship rather than a complete reliability estimator. The comparison between LLM methods and OLS is also sensitive to prompt design, calibration, batch formatting, and model version. Finally, the notebooks use log-view prediction, which is appropriate for heavy-tailed outcomes but does not directly optimize business metrics such as ranking, revenue, or hit detection.

## 6. Conclusion

This paper argues that numeric prediction from language should be evaluated through anisotropic semantic metrics. In task-specific inference, dimensions are not equally valuable. They have signs, weights, and channel-dependent meanings. The experiment shows that guided generation, which provides qualitative hints about the most significant dimensions, achieved the strongest LLM prediction performance. It also shows that better labeling of latent dimensions correlated with better numeric accuracy, suggesting that representation-level fidelity can help estimate reasoning quality even when ground truth is unavailable. At the same time, the strongest correlation with the OLS numeric model came from dimension prompting, raising the question of when LLMs can improve on linear semantic models rather than merely reproduce them.

The conceptual contribution is to treat prompts as metric-shaping devices. A prompt selects an anisotropic topology over semantic space, emphasizing some dimensions, suppressing others, and assigning signs to directions. This view connects numeric prediction, classification, retrieval, summarization, and long-context generation under a common framework: language is not only content, but a mechanism for constructing evaluation geometry. The next step is to move from static anisotropic metrics to trajectory metrics, where the quality of reasoning is measured by the path a generation follows through semantic space.
