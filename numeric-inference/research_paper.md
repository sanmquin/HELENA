# Semantic Topology and Anisotropic Metrics: Navigating Latent Gradients in Non-Verifiable Numeric Prediction

## Abstract

Predicting quantitative outcomes from high-dimensional qualitative data remains a foundational challenge in machine learning, particularly in domains where ground-truth is probabilistic, emergent from behavioral patterns, or inherently non-verifiable. This paper investigates the prediction of YouTube video popularity—measured by logarithmic views—as a function of the semantic properties of video titles. We propose a framework rooted in semantic topology, arguing for a shift away from isotropic evaluation metrics such as cosine similarity toward anisotropic metrics that account for the task-specific importance of individual latent dimensions. Through a series of extensive experiments involving per-channel Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) baselines and four distinct Large Language Model (LLM) reasoning strategies, we demonstrate three central empirical results. First, guided inference, which incorporates qualitative "hints" derived from statistical significance, achieves superior predictive accuracy compared to zero-shot or exhaustive dimensional approaches, yielding a normalized Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 0.959 and an R-squared of 0.191. Second, we establish that the accuracy of an LLM’s qualitative "labeling" of latent dimensions (Mean Absolute Label Error) is a robust predictor of its final numeric precision, providing a methodology for reasoning evaluation even in the absence of absolute ground-truth labels. Third, we identify a correlation-bias paradox, where models focused on exhaustive dimensional analysis capture the statistical "shape" of the distribution with high fidelity (Pearson r = 0.912) but suffer from absolute calibration biases that only guided reasoning can mitigate. These findings suggest that the prompt functions as a topological reasoning bridge, enabling models to navigate the anisotropic gradients of semantic space by weighting latent dimensions according to their empirical relevance.

## 1. Introduction: From Isotropic Similarity to Anisotropic Gradients

The evaluation of high-dimensional embeddings has historically relied on isotropic metrics, most notably cosine similarity and Euclidean distance, which operate under the assumption that all dimensions in a vector space contribute equally to semantic distance. In a perfectly isotropic space, the relationship between any two semantic points is defined solely by their geometric distance and angle, regardless of the specific direction of the vector. While this assumption is often sufficient for broad information retrieval, zero-shot clustering, or generic similarity matching, it fails in specific predictive contexts where the "pull" or "influence" of different semantic features on a target variable is highly unequal. In the context of video popularity prediction, for example, a dimension representing "high-stakes storytelling" or "urgent news reporting" may exert a significantly stronger influence on audience engagement than a dimension representing "grammatical complexity" or "lexical diversity." We characterize such predictive spaces as anisotropic, meaning their topological properties—specifically the gradients of the target variable—vary drastically depending on the direction of movement within the latent space.

This paper frames numeric prediction in semantic spaces as a fundamentally topological problem. If a category description in a text classification task acts as a map to a decision boundary, then a performance driver in a numeric task acts as a map to a gradient. To understand why a model succeeds or fails in this environment, we must move beyond asking where a point is located in the space and begin asking how the model perceives the slope of the surface it is navigating. This perspective is particularly critical in non-verifiable domains, where the target variable is influenced by complex, latent behavioral factors that cannot be reduced to simple keyword matching or surface-level sentiment analysis. The challenge for the model is not merely to find the "nearest neighbor" in a training set but to understand the "gravity" of the semantic features that drive emergent human behavior. The topological lens allows us to treat the latent space not as a flat table of features, but as a curved landscape of influence where different axes of meaning have different weights.

To account for the performance degradation often observed when Large Language Models (LLMs) encounter narrow, channel-specific distributions, we introduce the concept of proactive interference. Borrowed from cognitive psychology, proactive interference refers to the tendency of previously learned information to hinder the acquisition and application of new, related data. In the context of Large Language Models, the vast, general-purpose training data acts as a pervasive source of interference. When asked to predict the success of a video title, a pre-trained model often defaults to global, generic success drivers—such as "use clickbait" or "optimize for length"—rather than grasping the unique anisotropic weights that define a specific channel’s niche audience. For a specialized academic channel, "sensationalism" might be a highly negative dimension that alienates viewers, whereas for a tabloid channel, it is the primary driver of growth. We argue that effective inference requires a "reasoning bridge"—a structured, task-specific prompt that overrides this interference by explicitly highlighting the significant topological features and gradients of the local semantic space. This bridge allows the model to "reset" its expectations and align its internal reasoning with the empirical reality of the specific dataset it is analyzing.

## 2. Methodology: A Sequential Pipeline for Latent Reasoning

Our experimental framework utilized a sequential pipeline designed to bridge the gap between statistical benchmarks and qualitative LLM reasoning. The primary dataset consisted of video titles, metadata, and view counts from several hundred YouTube channels, representing a diverse range of content from education and technology to entertainment and news. To ensure the relevance and temporal consistency of the data, we filtered the corpus to include only long-form content published between 2023 and 2025. This exclusion of "Shorts" and older videos was necessary because audience behavior and platform algorithms often follow different performance patterns for different formats and eras. Furthermore, we only retained channels with at least 200 videos after filtering to ensure a sufficient sample size for per-channel statistical modeling. To ensure the robustness of our final analysis, we focused our evaluation on ten "significant channels"—those where a linear model could explain a substantial portion of the variance in views based on title semantics alone, as determined by the F-statistic p-value of their per-channel Ordinary Least Squares models.

The process began with the generation of high-dimensional embeddings using the `all-MiniLM-L6-v2` sentence transformer, which maps titles into a 384-dimensional vector space. While 384 dimensions allow for a rich representation of meaning, such a space is too high-dimensional for direct qualitative description or efficient linear modeling with limited data. To make this semantic space tractable, we applied Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to identify the axes of greatest variance within each channel’s content distribution. We reduced the embeddings to a 15-dimensional subspace, a configuration we termed `GLOBAL_PCA_COMPONENTS`. While this reduction necessarily discards some high-frequency noise and very specific semantic nuances, it preserves the primary axes of semantic variation that drive large-scale audience behavior. For each channel, we split the data into 80% training and 20% testing sets. We then trained an Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression model on the log-transformed views (`log1p(views)`) to account for the long-tailed nature of audience engagement. This model served two critical purposes: it provided a numeric baseline for predictive accuracy and it identified the coefficients and p-values for each of the 15 dimensions, effectively mapping the anisotropic topology of the channel and identifying which dimensions truly "pulled" the view counts.

Parallel to the statistical modeling, we employed the Gemini 3.1 Flash lite model to generate qualitative interpretations of the latent space. This was achieved through a MECE-compliant framework, standing for Mutually Exclusive and Collectively Exhaustive. For each of the 15 dimensions, the model analyzed the 50 titles with the highest positive scores and the 50 titles with the highest negative scores. The model was then tasked with defining the semantic contrast represented by that axis, ensuring that the definitions did not overlap and covered the full range of meaning present in the subspace. For example, Dimension 8 might be defined as "Direct How-To Instructions vs. Philosophical Explorations." This step was crucial as it translated abstract numeric coordinates into a linguistic landscape that the LLM could navigate during the inference phase. By anchoring the dimensions in real examples, we ensured that the qualitative definitions were grounded in the actual data distribution rather than hallucinated from the model's pre-existing biases.

The final stage of the methodology involved the comparative evaluation of four distinct LLM-based prediction strategies, each designed to test a different level of topological awareness and reasoning depth. The Global LLM strategy provided the model with only high-level channel drivers—such as the channel’s niche, intended audience, and overall success patterns—and requested direct view predictions. The Dimension LLM strategy expanded this by providing the model with the precise qualitative definitions for all 15 PCA dimensions, asking the model to explicitly consider the contribution of each dimension in its prediction. The Feature Evaluation LLM introduced a structured, two-step reasoning process. Before making a numeric prediction, the model was required to label the qualitative contribution of each dimension on a 7-point scale—ranging from "extremely negative" (-3) to "extremely positive" (+3). These labels were mapped to specific percentiles of the training distribution, with "extremely positive" representing the top 5% of contributions. Finally, the Guided Inference strategy provided the model with qualitative "hints" for only the five most significant dimensions, as identified by the OLS p-values. These hints directly mapped the video’s PCA coordinates to the 7-point scale, effectively "handing" the model the most important gradients in the semantic space while reducing the cognitive noise from non-significant dimensions.

## 3. Results: The Geometry of Predictive Reasoning and the Impact of Qualitative Fidelity

The evaluation across the ten significant channels revealed a complex and illuminating relationship between qualitative reasoning and quantitative accuracy. Because different channels operate at vastly different scales of popularity—with some averaging thousands of views and others millions—all results were normalized by subtracting the channel’s central tendency calculated from the training data. This normalization was performed using either the mean or the median, depending on the distribution's skew, allowing for a unified dataset-level evaluation of Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and the coefficient of determination (R²). This process ensured that our metrics reflected the model's ability to predict relative popularity within a channel's context rather than its ability to guess the absolute scale of a channel's reach.

The first major result is the clear and consistent superiority of the Guided Inference strategy. With a normalized MAE of 0.959 and an R² of 0.191, the Guided Inference model significantly outperformed all other LLM approaches. For comparison, the Global LLM achieved an MAE of 1.012 and an R² of 0.085, while the Dimension LLM, despite having access to more information, performed worse with an MAE of 1.058 and an R² of 0.075. While the OLS numeric model remains the performance ceiling with an MAE of 0.924 and an R² of 0.264, the improvement from the Global approach to the Guided approach represents a significant leap in both accuracy and reliability. This confirms the core hypothesis: numeric inference in semantic spaces is not a simple zero-shot retrieval or pattern-matching task but a topological navigation task that is vastly improved by anisotropic weighting. By focusing the model’s attention on the specific gradients with the highest empirical "pull," we override the proactive interference of general-purpose knowledge and enable more precise and calibrated test-time reasoning. The model no longer has to guess which features matter; it is told which dimensions are the drivers of success.

The second major result establishes a direct link between the accuracy of latent representations and predictive outcomes, a phenomenon we term the qualitative-quantitative correlation. In the Feature Evaluation experiment, we calculated the Mean Absolute Label Error (MALE) by comparing the LLM’s qualitative dimension labels against the ground truth labels derived from the OLS models. We observed a strong and statistically significant correlation between MALE and the final prediction error at the individual video level. Videos where the model correctly identified the qualitative contribution of significant dimensions (i.e., low MALE) were predicted with much higher precision. Conversely, when the model "misunderstood" the role of a significant dimension—for instance, labeling a positive driver as neutral or negative—its numeric predictions drifted significantly from the actual views. This finding has profound implications for the evaluation of AI in non-verifiable domains where ground truth may be unavailable in real-time. It suggests that we can evaluate the accuracy of a model’s reasoning process by measuring the fidelity of its latent representations. If a model can correctly "label" the semantic drivers of a system, its quantitative predictions are likely to be reliable, even if the absolute numbers are difficult to verify.

The third result, which we characterize as the correlation-bias paradox, emerged from a deep comparison of the Dimension LLM and the Guided Inference LLM. Interestingly, the Dimension LLM—which analyzed all 15 dimensions in a zero-shot manner—achieved the highest Pearson correlation with the OLS numeric baseline (r = 0.912), compared to 0.902 for the Guided model. However, despite this higher correlation, its absolute error (MAE) was higher and its R² was significantly worse. This indicates that while exhaustive dimensional analysis allows the LLM to capture the "shape" of the statistical distribution with high fidelity—it knows which titles should be higher or lower relative to each other—the cognitive load and complexity of processing 15 dimensions simultaneously introduces significant absolute bias and variance. The model captures the relative ordering perfectly but fails on the absolute scale, often over-predicting or under-predicting the magnitude of the effects. Guided reasoning, by contrast, sacrifices a marginal amount of "perfect shape" to achieve a much better-calibrated absolute prediction. This poses a fundamental question for future research into the cognitive limits of LLM reasoning: to what extent can models improve upon simple linear models by leveraging non-linear relationships without falling into the "complexity trap" that introduces systematic bias.

## 4. Discussion: Toward Geometrically Faithful Models and Trans-Domain Applications

The results presented in this paper argue for a fundamental shift in how we approach Large Language Model-based prediction, reasoning, and evaluation. The prompt should no longer be viewed merely as a set of instructions, a collection of context, or a repository of examples. Instead, it must be understood as a topological reasoning bridge that aligns the model’s internal weights with the anisotropic gradients of the task-specific semantic space. This paradigm shift has significant implications for how we train models, how we perform knowledge distillation, and how we apply AI to complex, non-verifiable tasks across various industries.

### 4.1 Implications for Training, Distillation, and Test-Time Reasoning

The performance gap between the various LLM strategies and the OLS baseline suggests that current models, while powerful, are still under-utilizing the rich semantic information available in latent spaces. One promising path forward is the implementation of on-policy distillation or self-distillation techniques, where a "teacher" model—guided by the topological insights and significance metrics developed in this research—provides a dense, token-level reward signal to a "student" model. Instead of rewarding a model simply for arriving at a correct numeric prediction, which can often happen through spurious correlations, the reward should be tied to the structural fidelity of the reasoning path itself. A model that correctly identifies the significant dimensions and their relative "pull" should be weighted much more heavily than a model that arrives at the correct number through an inconsistent, contradictory, or "hallucinated" reasoning path.

This approach encourages the development of "geometrically faithful" models that internalize the topology of the data they are processing. By forcing a model to align its qualitative descriptions with the empirical gradients of the data during the training phase, we can systematically reduce the interference of general-purpose knowledge and improve the model's ability to generalize to unseen, narrow distributions. This is particularly relevant for the challenge of long-context generation, where maintaining topological cohesion across thousands of claims, facts, or concepts is a major hurdle for current architectures. In such settings, the model must maintain a coherent and consistent relationship between many disparate "points" in semantic space, a task that is essentially a large-scale, multi-dimensional version of the numeric prediction problem studied here. The ability to navigate these gradients without drifting into unrelated semantic neighborhoods is the key to maintaining long-form coherence.

### 4.2 Comparative Analysis: Classification Boundaries vs. Numeric Gradients

It is instructive to compare the findings of this numeric prediction research with the previous experiments conducted in the domain of text classification. In the classification experiment, the primary topological metric was distance preservation—measuring how well a generated category description preserved the semantic distance between category centroids. Success in that context was defined by the model’s ability to respect the boundaries and margins between distinct regions of the point cloud. The goal was to avoid "collapse" or "overlap" between categories in the description space. In the numeric prediction task, however, the challenge is more continuous, directional, and subtle. we are less concerned with the distance between discrete categories and more concerned with the gradient—the "slope"—within a single, continuous semantic field.

This distinction highlights the natural evolution of the semantic topology framework. While classification is essentially a problem of "location" and "boundaries"—determining which side of a line a point falls on—numeric prediction is a problem of "direction" and "magnitude." A model that truly understands the topology of a YouTube channel must not only know where a specific video title sits in the 15-dimensional embedding space but must also understand the local "gravity" of the popularity surface at that specific coordinate. The transition from distance-preservation metrics used in classification to the anisotropic weighting and gradient navigation used in numeric prediction represents a move from static topological mapping to dynamic process evaluation. This evolution suggests that evaluation in non-verifiable domains must become increasingly directional, measuring not just where a model "is" in its understanding but where its reasoning "points."

### 4.3 Applications in Non-Verifiable Domains and Beyond Entertainment

The practical applications of this research extend far beyond the niche of YouTube analytics and video titles. In any domain where qualitative communication—language, tone, narrative—drives quantitative outcomes, the ability to map and navigate latent gradients is a powerful asset. In the sales and marketing domains, for instance, the effectiveness of a brand message or a sales pitch is often a function of how well its semantic content aligns with the latent "pull" of the target audience’s values, fears, and needs. By treating a marketing campaign as a point in an anisotropic semantic space, we can estimate its potential impact long before deployment by measuring its alignment with proven success dimensions and gradients. In sales, the "trajectory" of a conversation—the sequence of semantic dimensions explored by the salesperson—can be more predictive of a deal’s success than any single keyword or sentiment score.

Similar logic applies to high-stakes domains such as education, strategic communication, and personal self-development. In the field of education, identifying the specific semantic dimensions of a learning material—such as its "abstractness" vs. "concreteness"—that correlate with student engagement and retention can help in the design of more effective and personalized pedagogical tools. In the realm of personal self-development, an AI model could analyze the semantic dimensions of a person's recorded habits, goals, or reflections to predict long-term outcomes, using guided inference to overcome the proactive interference of generic, "one-size-fits-all" advice. Ultimately, any task that involves "reasoning under uncertainty" in a high-dimensional space can be reformulated and solved as a problem of navigating anisotropic gradients. The framework provides a bridge between the fuzzy world of language and the precise world of numeric outcomes.

## 5. Conclusion: The Path Toward Trajectory Similarity

This paper has demonstrated that semantic topology and anisotropic metrics provide a robust and principled foundation for numeric prediction in non-verifiable domains. The core finding—that guided inference and qualitative labeling accuracy are central to predictive success—points toward a future where AI evaluation is centered on the structural fidelity of the reasoning process rather than just the surface-level accuracy of the final outputs. We have shown that by accounting for the inherent anisotropy of latent spaces and using "reasoning bridges" to overcome the proactive interference of general-purpose data, we can build models that are better calibrated, more reliable, and more resistant to the systemic biases of current Large Language Model architectures.

The immediate next step in this research program is the development of a formal metric based on trajectory similarity. While the current work focuses on the "pull" of individual dimensions at a single, static point in space, most complex reasoning and decision-making tasks involve a sequence of inferences—a path or "trajectory" through the semantic field. We propose to measure the similarity between the "reasoning trajectory" generated by an LLM during a multi-step task and the actual data trajectories observed in successful real-world outcomes. This would move the framework from evaluating static state-matching to evaluating dynamic process-verification. If we can show that models following a specific topological path are consistently more accurate and robust, we can begin to formalize a "law of semantic motion" that governs how models should navigate latent spaces to achieve optimal reasoning performance. Ultimately, the goal is to create AI systems that don't just "guess" the right answer, but "reason" their way through the latent gradients of the world with geometric precision, topological awareness, and verifiable structural fidelity.
